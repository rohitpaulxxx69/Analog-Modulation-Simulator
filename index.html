Modulation Formulas & Mathematical Model 🧮

1. Message Signal, $m(t)$:

$$m(t) = A_m \cos(2\pi f_m t)$$

2. Carrier Signal, $c(t)$:

$$c(t) = A_c \cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

3. Amplitude Modulated (AM) Signal, $s(t)$:

$$s(t) = A_c \left[1 + m \cos(2\pi f_m t)\right] \cos(2\pi f_c t)$$

Amplitude Modulation (AM) DSB-FC

Amplitude Modulation (AM) is a technique used in electronic communication, most commonly for transmitting information via a radio carrier wave. In AM, the amplitude (signal strength) of the carrier wave is varied in proportion to the instantaneous amplitude of the message (information) signal, such as an audio signal.

The equations represent a Double Sideband Large Carrier (DSB-LC) or Double Sideband Full Carrier (DSB-FC) modulation scheme. This is the traditional AM broadcasting system.

Modulation Index ($m$)

The modulation index $m$ (also called modulation depth) is a measure of the extent of modulation. It represents the ratio of the amplitude of the message signal ($A_m$) to the carrier amplitude ($A_c$): $$m = \frac{A_m}{A_c}$$

For distortionless recovery of the message using simple envelope detectors, the modulation index should satisfy $0 \le m \le 1$.

Power in AM Signal

The total power $P_t$ of an AM signal consists of the carrier power $P_c$ and sidebands power $P_{sb}$ (upper and lower sidebands): $$P_t = P_c \left(1 + \frac{m^2}{2}\right)$$ The sidebands contain all the useful information. The efficiency $\eta$ represents the percentage of total power that contains the actual information: $$\eta = \frac{P_{sb}}{P_t} = \frac{m^2}{2 + m^2} \times 100\%$$ For a modulation index of $m = 1.0$, the efficiency reaches its maximum of $\approx 33.3\%$. The remaining $66.7\%$ of the transmitted power resides in the carrier wave itself, which carries no information but is crucial for simple receiver detection.

Understanding Overmodulation ($m > 1$)

When the message signal amplitude $A_m$ exceeds the carrier amplitude $A_c$, the modulation index $m$ becomes greater than $1$. This condition is known as overmodulation.

In the time domain, you can observe that the lower envelope of the modulated wave crosses the zero-voltage axis and becomes positive, while the upper envelope crosses below zero. This creates a phase reversal in the carrier signal at the points of envelope crossing:

  • Envelope Distortion: A simple diode envelope detector (commonly used in inexpensive AM receivers) cannot follow the phase reversals. It will instead rectify the signal and follow the absolute value of the envelope, producing a heavily distorted output representing $|1 + m\cos(2\pi f_m t)|$ rather than the original message.
  • Sideband Splatter: In the frequency domain, the abrupt phase transitions of overmodulation generate high-frequency components that spread far beyond the allocated bandwidth. This causes interference in adjacent radio channels, termed "sideband splatter".

To prevent overmodulation, transmitters use automatic level controls (limiters) to ensure the peak message voltage never exceeds the carrier amplitude.

Practical Applications of AM

Although newer, more bandwidth- and power-efficient modulation methods have been developed (such as FM, SSB, and digital schemes), Amplitude Modulation is still widely used due to the simplicity of transmitter and receiver circuits.

  • AM Broadcasting: Medium Wave (MW) and Short Wave (SW) bands are still active worldwide, providing news and entertainment over hundreds of miles due to ionospheric reflection.
  • Airband Communications: VHF radio bands ($108 - 137 \text{ MHz}$) for civil aviation use amplitude modulation. AM exhibits the "capture effect" less than FM, allowing two transmitters on the same frequency to be heard simultaneously (a pilot can hear an overriding emergency call rather than one signal fully blocking the other).
  • QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation): Modern high-speed digital communications (such as Wi-Fi, cable internet, and LTE/5G cellular networks) combine AM with Phase Modulation (PSK) to encode multiple bits per symbol, maximizing spectral efficiency.
